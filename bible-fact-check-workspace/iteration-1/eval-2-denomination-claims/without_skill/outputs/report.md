## 2. Factual Errors

### 2a. Line 80 -- Catholic Claim 1: "Original sin is Augustine's concept (4-5C)"
- **Oversimplification**: Augustine systematized the doctrine, but the idea of inherited fallenness has roots in intertestamental literature (e.g., 4 Ezra 3:21-22, 7:118; 2 Baruch 54:15-19). Saying it is purely "Augustine's concept" is misleading. The entry should acknowledge pre-Augustinian antecedents.

### 2b. Line 85 -- Catholic Claim 2: "formally defined 553 CE"
- **Imprecise**: The Second Council of Constantinople (553 CE) did not formally define the perpetual virginity of Mary as a dogma. It used the title "ever-virgin" (aeiparthenos) in passing as an already-accepted epithet. The teaching was affirmed much earlier (e.g., by Jerome, Ambrose, and Augustine in the 4th century, and the Lateran Council of 649 under Pope Martin I). Calling 553 CE the date of formal definition is misleading.

### 2c. Line 122 -- Charismatic Claim 1: "Joel 3:1 uses nava"
- **Verse reference issue**: Joel 3:1 in Hebrew (MT) numbering = Joel 2:28 in English numbering. The file does not clarify which numbering system it uses, creating potential confusion. Since the file is in English, Joel 2:28 would be the expected reference. Alternatively, note the MT numbering explicitly.

### 2d. Line 202 -- Easter: "Eostre = Germanic dawn goddess"
- The claim that "Eostre is unrelated to Ishtar" is correct. However, the evidence for Eostre as a Germanic goddess is thin -- our sole source is Bede (725 CE, De Temporum Ratione). The entry presents this as settled fact without noting the thinness of evidence for Eostre herself.

### 2e. Line 256-258 -- Hinduism Claim 1: "Bhagavata Purana dates to 800-1000 CE (some scholars: as early as 6C)"
- **Oversimplification of dating**: The parenthetical "some scholars: as early as 6C" is vague. Most mainstream scholarship places final compilation around 9th-10th century CE. The "as early as 6C" view is a minority position and should be flagged as such more clearly, or simply omitted.

### 2f. Line 313 -- JW Claim 1: "Colwell 1933, Harner 1973"
- **Conflation**: E.C. Colwell's rule (1933) is often misapplied. Colwell's rule states that a definite predicate nominative preceding the verb usually lacks the article -- it does NOT prove that an anarthrous predicate nominative IS definite. Philip Harner (1973) argued the construction is *qualitative*, which is the correct point. The entry conflates them by listing both together as if they support the same conclusion.

### 2g. Line 328 -- JW Claim 4: "helon (nails, plural)"
- **Minor Greek error**: The form in John 20:25 is ton typon ton helon (genitive plural of helos, "nail"). The transliteration "helon" is acceptable but should be noted as genitive plural, not nominative.

### 2h. Line 352 -- Marketplace/Seven Mountain: "coined 1975 (Bright/Cunningham)"
- **Imprecise**: The 1975 date refers to the alleged simultaneous visions of Bill Bright and Loren Cunningham. However, the specific "Seven Mountain Mandate" terminology was not coined until Lance Wallnau popularized it around 2000-2013. "Coined 1975" is too strong.

### 2i. Line 502 -- Unification Claim 1: "Satan as fallen angel theology develops later (intertestamental period)"
- **Contradictory chronology**: The entry says "No 1C Jewish source reads Gen 3 as sexual fall" and then says the Satan-as-fallen-angel theology "develops later (intertestamental period, systematized in Christianity)." The intertestamental period (roughly 3rd century BCE to 1st century CE) is *before or contemporaneous with* the 1C, not "later." 1 Enoch and the Book of Jubilees already develop angelic fall narratives. The phrasing "develops later" is confusing.

### 2j. Line 164 -- Daoism Claim 1: "translation to Chinese 'Dao' (1919 CUV)"
- **Inaccurate implication**: The Chinese Union Version (CUV, 1919) did use "Dao" for Logos, but earlier Chinese translations (Morrison 1823, Delegates Version 1854) also used 道. The 1919 date implies the CUV originated the connection, which is historically inaccurate.

## 4. Anachronisms in Analysis

### 4a. Line 71 -- Calvinism: "yetzer (good/evil inclination) framework"
- The entry correctly identifies that the 1C Jewish framework was yetzer, not total depravity. However, the yetzer ha-tov as a distinct concept paired with yetzer ha-ra is a later rabbinic development (Talmudic period). The 1C evidence primarily discusses the yetzer ha-ra. Calling it a "good/evil inclination" framework oversimplifies the 1C picture.

### 4b. Line 146 -- Evangelical: "Trinity defined 325-381 CE"
- The Council of Nicaea (325) defined the Son's relationship to the Father (homoousios), while Constantinople (381) addressed the Holy Spirit. The full Trinitarian formulation was refined over centuries. The range 325-381 is reasonable shorthand but somewhat oversimplified.

## 5. Oversimplifications

### 5a. Lines 69-70 -- Calvinism: "Romans 9's 'Jacob and Esau' = two nations, not two individuals' eternal destinies"
- While the national/corporate reading is strong, the entry dismisses the individual reading too flatly. Paul's potter/clay imagery in Romans 9:19-23 does address individual divine sovereignty in some interpretive traditions. The entry should note this is debated.

### 5b. Line 262 -- Hinduism Claim 2: "hen (neuter 'one') rules out personal identity"
- The entry then says "whether hen indicates shared purpose, shared nature, or both is debated among scholars," which contradicts the initial flat statement. The qualifying sentence is more accurate; the opening claim is too strong.

### 5c. Line 448 -- Sola Fide: "erga nomou (works of law) in 1C = Jewish identity markers"
- This reflects the New Perspective on Paul (Sanders, Dunn, Wright), presented as settled fact. Traditional Protestant and many other scholars read erga nomou more broadly. The entry should present this as a scholarly perspective, not the definitive 1C reading.

### 5d. Lines 360-361 -- Mashiach Concept: "mashiach never means 'God incarnate' in Tanakh"
- Technically accurate for the Hebrew Bible's own usage, but Daniel 7:13-14 (the "son of man" receives worship) and Psalm 45:6 (the king addressed as "elohim") show the question of divine attributes for a messianic figure is genuinely debated. The flat denial oversimplifies.

### 5e. Line 392 -- Mormonism Claim 1: "reductio ad absurdum argument"
- Describing Paul's reference to baptism for the dead as a "reductio ad absurdum" is one interpretation, but far from consensus. Many scholars see Paul referencing an actual practice without endorsing it, using it rhetorically. Over 40 interpretations exist (as the entry itself notes), so picking one and stating it flatly is inconsistent.
