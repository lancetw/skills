## Claim 1: Trinity formulated at Nicaea (325 CE) and Constantinople (381 CE). The word "Trinity" never appears in the Bible.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- The word "Trinity" (Greek: trias; Latin: trinitas) does not appear anywhere in the Bible. CONFIRMED.
- However, saying the Trinity was "formulated at Nicaea" is an overstatement. The Council of Nicaea (325 CE) addressed only the relationship between the Father and the Son (against Arianism). It said almost nothing about the Holy Spirit. The Nicene Creed of 325 is considered binitarian, not fully Trinitarian.
- The Council of Constantinople (381 CE) expanded the creed to include the Holy Spirit as "worshiped and glorified together with the Father and the Son," completing the Trinitarian formulation. CONFIRMED.
- The Latin term "trinitas" was coined much earlier by Tertullian (c. 200 CE), and Trinitarian thought existed before Nicaea. The councils formalized and dogmatized it, but did not invent it from nothing.

**Correction needed:** Nicaea did not formulate the Trinity -- it addressed the Father-Son relationship. Constantinople completed the Trinitarian formula. The claim should say "the full Trinitarian doctrine was progressively defined at Nicaea (325, Father-Son) and Constantinople (381, Holy Spirit)."

## Claim 2: echad in Deuteronomy 6:4 means numerically one, not "compound unity" -- the compound-unity claim is a modern apologetic invention not found in any ancient Hebrew grammar.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that echad is the standard Hebrew cardinal number "one." All major Hebrew lexicons (BDB, HALOT, etc.) give its primary meaning as the numeral one. The sense of plurality in phrases like "one flesh" (Genesis 2:24) comes from the collective noun modified by echad, not from echad itself.
- CONFIRMED that no ancient Hebrew grammar or lexicon defines echad as "compound unity." The compound-unity argument does not appear in classical Jewish commentaries or standard lexicography.
- However, calling it a "modern apologetic invention" is somewhat imprecise. The echad/yachid distinction in Trinitarian apologetics became popular in the 20th century (particularly through evangelical and Messianic Jewish apologetics), but some earlier Christian writers used similar reasoning. The key point stands: the "compound unity" meaning is not derived from Hebrew linguistics but from theological argumentation.
- Medieval Jewish philosopher Maimonides argued for echad as absolute simplicity (yachid-like oneness), which was a philosophical refinement, not a concession to compound-unity readings.

**Correction needed:** The core linguistic claim is correct, but "modern apologetic invention" could be softened to "a theological argument not supported by standard Hebrew lexicography or ancient grammars" for greater precision.

## Claim 3: John 10:30 uses neuter hen (unity of purpose), not masculine heis (identity).

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that the Greek word in John 10:30 is the neuter hen, not the masculine heis. This is a verifiable grammatical fact from the Greek text.
- CONFIRMED that if Jesus meant personal identity with the Father, the masculine heis would have been expected.
- However, the parenthetical gloss "unity of purpose" is one interpretation but not the only scholarly reading. Trinitarian scholars argue that neuter hen indicates unity of essence/nature/substance (not personal identity, but shared divine nature), not merely unity of purpose. The neuter form rules out "same person" (Sabellianism) but does not automatically reduce the meaning to "unity of purpose" alone.
- The same neuter hen appears in 1 Corinthians 3:8 ("he who plants and he who waters are one") for cooperative purpose, but context determines meaning in each case.

**Correction needed:** The grammatical observation (neuter hen, not masculine heis) is correct. But glossing hen as specifically "unity of purpose" is an interpretive choice, not a grammatical conclusion. The claim should present the grammatical fact and note the interpretive debate rather than asserting one interpretation as settled.

## Claim 4: Sunday worship became official via Constantine (321 CE) and Council of Laodicea (~364 CE).

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that Constantine issued a civil law in 321 CE making Sunday a day of rest from work (except for farming). This was the first civil/legal enforcement of Sunday rest.
- CONFIRMED that the Council of Laodicea (c. 363-364 CE) issued canons discouraging Sabbath-keeping and mandating Sunday as the primary day of worship. The council stated Christians should not "Judaize" by resting on the Sabbath.
- However, the claim that Sunday worship "became official" only through these events is misleading. Sunday worship was already widespread among Christians well before Constantine:
  - Acts 20:7 and 1 Corinthians 16:2 reference first-day gatherings.
  - The Didache (c. 70-120 CE) mentions "the Lord's Day" gathering.
  - Ignatius of Antioch (c. 110 CE), the Epistle of Barnabas (c. 130 CE), and Justin Martyr (c. 155 CE) all attest to Sunday worship.
  - By the late second century, Sunday worship was universal and uncontested in orthodox Christianity.
- Constantine made it a civil law; Laodicea addressed lingering Sabbath observance in the East. Neither invented Sunday worship.

**Correction needed:** The claim should say "Sunday rest was first mandated as civil law by Constantine (321 CE), and the Council of Laodicea (~364 CE) formally prohibited Sabbath-keeping." It should not imply these events originated Sunday worship, which predated them by centuries.

Sources:
- [Sabbath Facts - Constantine Sunday Law 321](https://sabbathfacts.org/constantine-sunday-law-321/)

## Claim 5: Sola fide formulated by Luther in the 16th century. Luther added "allein" to Romans 3:28 in his German translation.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that Luther added the word "allein" (alone) to his German translation of Romans 3:28, which reads "allein durch den Glauben" (alone through faith). The Greek text does not contain the word "alone" (monon). Luther himself acknowledged this in his 1530 "Open Letter on Translating," arguing that German idiom required the addition for clarity and that the theological context demanded it.
- However, saying sola fide was "formulated by Luther" oversimplifies the history:
  - Luther himself cited Ambrose and Augustine as predecessors.
  - Some church fathers (e.g., Clement of Rome, Chrysostom) made statements that appear to support justification by faith, though their full theology included works as part of the Christian life.
  - The Protestant historian Alister McGrath notes that Luther's specific formulation of forensic/extrinsic justification (declared righteous, not made righteous) was genuinely new compared to Augustine's intrinsic justification.
- The formal doctrine of sola fide as a systematic theological principle was indeed crystallized by Luther and the Reformation, even if earlier thinkers expressed related ideas.

**Correction needed:** The "allein" addition is factually correct. But "formulated by Luther" should be nuanced to "systematized as a formal doctrine by Luther," since faith-based justification ideas existed earlier in less developed forms.

Sources:
- [Sola Fide - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sola_fide)
- [Heidelblog - Luther's Translation of Romans 3:28](https://heidelblog.net/2020/04/the-reformed-churches-confess-luthers-translation-of-romans-328-allein/)
- [WELS - Romans 3:28 and Martin Luther](https://wels.net/faq/romans-328-and-martin-luther/)

## Claim 6: Altar call / sinner's prayer is a 19th-century invention (Charles Finney ~1830s).

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that the altar call as a formalized practice emerged in the early 19th century and is strongly associated with Charles Finney's "New Measures" revivalism in the 1820s-1830s. Finney popularized the "anxious bench" (a precursor to the modern altar call).
- However, the mourner's bench (a related precursor) appeared earlier in Methodist circles. The first documented public invitation to sinners for prayer was recorded in a Maryland Methodist church on October 31, 1798. By 1800, mourner's benches were used in Methodist circuits.
- The sinner's prayer specifically is a LATER and SEPARATE invention. Finney did not use a sinner's prayer. The earliest printed sinner's prayer dates to 1922 (Albert Gage's "Evangelism of Youth"). It was popularized much later by Billy Graham and Bill Bright (Four Spiritual Laws).
- Bundling "altar call" and "sinner's prayer" together as one invention is inaccurate -- they have different origins, separated by roughly a century.

**Correction needed:** The altar call traces to the early 19th century (Finney, 1820s-1830s), but the mourner's bench predates Finney (1798 Methodists). The sinner's prayer is a 20th-century development (1922+), not 19th-century. These should be treated as separate practices with distinct histories.

Sources:
- [9Marks - Origins of the Altar Call and Sinner's Prayer](https://www.9marks.org/review/the-origins-of-the-altar-call-and-the-sinners-prayer/)
- [Altar Call - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altar_call)
- [Mourner's Bench - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mourner's_bench)

## Claim 7: Calvinism TULIP formulated at Dort (1618-1619).

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that the Synod of Dort met from November 13, 1618 to May 29, 1619 and produced the Canons of Dort in response to the Five Articles of Remonstrance (Arminian positions from 1610).
- CONFIRMED that the five theological points summarized by the TULIP acronym correspond to the Canons of Dort.
- However, the TULIP acronym itself was NOT created at Dort. It is a later English-language mnemonic. There is no certainty regarding the origin of the acronym. R.L. Dabney discussed "The Five Points of Calvinism" circa 1878, but the TULIP acronym appears to be a 20th-century development.
- Additionally, calling these "Calvinism" is somewhat misleading -- John Calvin died in 1564, decades before Dort. The five points were formulated by his theological successors in response to Arminianism. Calvin himself never articulated these exact five points.

**Correction needed:** The five doctrinal points were formulated at Dort (1618-1619) -- CONFIRMED. But the TULIP acronym was a later English-language creation, and the points represent post-Calvin Reformed theology, not Calvin's own systematic formulation.

## Claim 8: Hebrews 8:13 uses kainos (renewed/fresh), not neos (brand new replacement).

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that Hebrews 8:13 uses kainos (specifically the form Kainen / accusative singular feminine) and NOT neos. The Greek text reads: "en to legein Kainen pepalaioken ten proten." This is verifiable from any Greek interlinear.
- CONFIRMED that kainos and neos have different semantic ranges. Kainos emphasizes qualitative newness (new in kind/character), while neos emphasizes temporal newness (recently made/young).
- However, the parenthetical glosses need correction:
  - Kainos does NOT simply mean "renewed/fresh." Standard Greek lexicons (BDAG, Thayer, Liddell-Scott) define kainos as "new, unused, novel, previously unknown" -- emphasizing qualitative difference and superiority. While some argue it CAN carry the sense of "renewed," this is not its primary lexical meaning.
  - Neos does NOT simply mean "brand new replacement." It means "new in time, recent, young."
  - Interestingly, Hebrews 12:24 DOES use neos for the covenant in a different context, showing the author knew both words and chose deliberately.

**Correction needed:** The factual observation that Hebrews 8:13 uses kainos and not neos is correct. But glossing kainos as "renewed/fresh" overstates the "renewal" meaning. Standard lexicography defines kainos as qualitatively new/different, which is stronger than mere renewal. The claim imports a theological conclusion (covenant renewed, not replaced) into the lexical definition.

## Claim 9: Psalm 46:10 harpu means "stop fighting/drop your weapons" -- a military command.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that the Hebrew word in Psalm 46:10 is harpu (transliterated variously as harpu / harephu), from the root raphah. The form is a Hifil imperative plural.
- CONFIRMED that raphah can mean "sink down, relax, let go, cease striving, let drop, withdraw, slacken."
- CONFIRMED that in the military context of Psalm 46 (which speaks of wars, nations in uproar, kingdoms falling, God breaking bows and shattering spears), the meaning "stop fighting / cease striving" is well-supported.
- However, the specific gloss "drop your weapons" is an interpretive paraphrase, not a direct lexical meaning of raphah. Raphah means to slacken, let go, or cease -- which in a military context implies ceasing combat. The "drop your weapons" image is a vivid but loose rendering.
- Some scholars also note that raphah can come from a root meaning "to heal" or "to be weak/feeble," and the Hifil form could mean "cause to let go" in a broader sense (not exclusively military).
- Calling it exclusively "a military command" narrows the semantic range too much. It is a command to cease striving that, in this specific psalm's context, has strong military overtones.

**Correction needed:** The core claim that harpu means "stop fighting" (not "be quiet and meditative") is well-supported by the context and lexicography. But "drop your weapons" is an interpretive flourish, and calling it exclusively a "military command" overstates what the word itself means. Better: "In the military context of Psalm 46, harpu means 'cease striving / stop fighting,' not 'be still in quiet meditation.'"

## Claim 10: erga nomou appears in Dead Sea Scrolls 4QMMT as ma'asei ha-torah.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

- CONFIRMED that the Dead Sea Scroll document 4QMMT (Miqsat Ma'asei Ha-Torah), meaning "Some Precepts of the Torah" or "Some Works of the Law," contains the Hebrew phrase ma'asei ha-torah.
- CONFIRMED that the Greek phrase erga nomou ("works of the law") used by Paul (Galatians 2:16, 3:2, 3:5, 3:10; Romans 3:20, 3:28) is the Greek equivalent of the Hebrew ma'asei ha-torah.
- CONFIRMED that the phrase "works of the law" is found only in 4QMMT and in Paul's letters, making this a uniquely significant parallel.
- The document was composed c. 150 BCE and contains over twenty halakhic (legal) rulings concerning ritual purity, particularly related to the Jerusalem temple.
- Scholar Martin Abegg was the first to widely publicize this connection, arguing that Paul was rebutting the kind of theology found in documents like 4QMMT.

## Claim 11: The "Old/New Testament" division is a 2nd-century Marcionite-influenced editorial choice.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that the division into "Old" and "New" Testament is a 2nd-century development in terminology.
- CONFIRMED that Marcion (c. 85-160 CE, excommunicated c. 144 CE) was a major catalyst. Tertullian stated that Marcion was the first to separate the New Testament from the Old. Marcion published the earliest known canon of New Testament books.
- However, calling it simply a "Marcionite-influenced" choice is misleading:
  - Paul himself used the concept of "old covenant" vs. "new covenant" (2 Corinthians 3:6, 3:14) long before Marcion.
  - Melito of Sardis (c. 170 CE) is the first known writer to use the term "Old Testament" for the collection of books, and he was an orthodox bishop, not a Marcionite.
  - Tertullian (c. 200 CE) coined the Latin "Novum Testamentum" and "Vetus Testamentum."
  - The orthodox church's adoption of the two-testament terminology was partly a response to Marcion (to assert that both testaments were Scripture, contra Marcion who rejected the Old), not an adoption of his framework.
- Marcion wanted to REJECT the Old Testament entirely. The orthodox response was to affirm BOTH testaments. The naming convention reflects anti-Marcionite orthodoxy as much as Marcionite influence.

**Correction needed:** "2nd-century" is correct. "Marcionite-influenced" is partially correct in that Marcion's challenge catalyzed the church's formalization of a two-testament canon. But the terminology was used by orthodox writers (not Marcionites) and the concept of old/new covenants predates Marcion (Paul). Better: "The 'Old/New Testament' terminology was formalized in the 2nd century, partly in response to Marcion's challenge, though the old/new covenant concept goes back to Paul."

## Claim 12: Seven Mountain Mandate invented in 1975 by Bill Bright and Loren Cunningham.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- CONFIRMED that in August 1975, Loren Cunningham (founder of YWAM) and Bill Bright (founder of Campus Crusade for Christ) met and shared lists of societal spheres they believed God wanted Christians to influence. Their lists were reportedly almost identical.
- CONFIRMED that Francis Schaeffer independently articulated similar ideas around the same time. A few weeks after the Cunningham-Bright meeting, Cunningham heard Schaeffer make a similar argument. However, Schaeffer was not present at the 1975 meeting.
- However, calling it the "Seven Mountain Mandate" in 1975 is anachronistic. In 1975, Cunningham and Bright spoke of "spheres of influence" or "classrooms" for discipleship and evangelism. Cunningham's original list had six items; a seventh was added later.
- The "Seven Mountain Mandate" terminology and its dominionist theology emerged later, particularly after a meeting between Cunningham and Lance Wallnau around 2000. The emphasis shifted from evangelism within cultural spheres to "taking dominion over" those spheres -- a significant theological shift driven by New Apostolic Reformation figures.
- The word "mandate" (implying dominion/control) was not part of the original 1975 concept.

**Correction needed:** The 1975 meeting between Bright and Cunningham is confirmed. But the "Seven Mountain Mandate" as a named concept with dominionist overtones was not invented in 1975 -- it was rebranded and radicalized c. 2000+. Better: "The concept of seven cultural spheres for Christian influence originated in 1975 with Bright and Cunningham; the 'Seven Mountain Mandate' as a dominionist framework was developed later (c. 2000s) by Lance Wallnau and others."

## Claim 13: Easter bunny first attested 1682 Germany.

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CORRECT**

- The Easter Hare (Osterhase) was documented by Georg Franck von Franckenau in his work "De ovis paschalibus" ("On Easter Eggs").
- Sources disagree on the exact date. Wikipedia's Easter Bunny article references both 1678 and 1682. Most popular sources (including Snopes and Mental Floss) cite 1682. Some scholarly sources cite 1678.
- The location is confirmed as south-west Germany (Heidelberg area, Protestant/Lutheran regions).
- CONFIRMED that this is the earliest known written attestation of the Easter Hare tradition. The tradition itself likely predates the written record.

**Correction needed:** The date is likely 1682 (publication date of De ovis paschalibus), though some sources say 1678. Either way, "1682 Germany" is approximately correct and consistent with the majority of sources. Minor discrepancy only.

## Claim 14: Grape juice (not wine) for communion is a 19th-century American temperance movement innovation (Welch, 1869).

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

- CONFIRMED that Thomas Bramwell Welch, a Methodist communion steward and dentist, developed pasteurized grape juice in 1869 specifically to provide an unfermented alternative to communion wine.
- CONFIRMED that this was directly motivated by the American temperance movement. In 1864, the Methodist Episcopal Church's General Conference recommended "the pure juice of the grape" for communion. Welch responded by using Pasteur's pasteurization technique to prevent grape juice from fermenting into wine.
- CONFIRMED that before pasteurization, unfermented grape juice could not be preserved (it would naturally ferment at room temperature; home refrigerators were not available until 1913). Therefore, wine was the only practical option for communion throughout most of Christian history.
- Welch initially marketed it as "Dr. Welch's Unfermented Wine." His son Charles later expanded the business and promoted it to churches. By 1916, Methodists made grape juice mandatory for communion.

## Claim 15: Jeremiah 31:31-33's "new covenant" (berit chadashah) puts Torah IN the heart -- it doesn't abolish Torah.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

- CONFIRMED that Jeremiah 31:33 explicitly states: "I will put My Torah within them, and on their hearts I will write it." The Hebrew text uses the word "torah" (instruction/law) -- the same word used throughout the Hebrew Bible for God's instruction.
- CONFIRMED that the passage describes Torah being internalized (moved from external tablets to the heart/inward parts), not abolished or replaced with something else.
- CONFIRMED that the Hebrew word chadashah (from chadash) can mean "new" or "renewed" -- as in the "new moon" (chodesh), which is the same moon renewed each month, not a different moon.
- CONFIRMED that Jewish interpretation consistently reads this passage as a renewed national commitment to God's Torah, not its abolition. The "newness" is in the mode of delivery (internal vs. external) and the depth of obedience, not in the content of the law being changed.
- The passage is the only place in the entire Hebrew Bible where the exact phrase "berit chadashah" (new covenant) appears.
