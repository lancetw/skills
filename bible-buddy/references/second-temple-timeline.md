# Second Temple Period Timeline: 586 BCE - 70 CE

Essential historical context for situating biblical texts, Second Temple literature, and first-century Judaism. All dates verified against standard references (Schurer, VanderKam, Collins).

---

## Table of Contents
- Persian Period (586-332 BCE)
- Hellenistic Period (332-167 BCE)
- Hasmonean Period (167-63 BCE)
- Roman Period (63 BCE-70 CE)

---

| Date | Event | Significance for Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **586 BCE** | Babylonian destruction of First Temple; Judean exile begins | Trauma behind Lamentations, exilic psalms, Jeremiah. Jer 29:11 addressed to THIS exile. |
| **539 BCE** | Cyrus the Great conquers Babylon; edict permits Jewish return (Ezra 1:1-4) | Isaiah 45:1 calls Cyrus mashiach (anointed one) -- a foreign king. Demonstrates mashiach is functional, not ethnic. |
| **520-516 BCE** | Second Temple constructed under Zerubbabel (Ezra 5-6; Haggai; Zechariah 1-8) | Modest structure; older priests wept comparing it to Solomon's Temple (Ezra 3:12). No Ark of the Covenant inside. |
| **458 BCE** | Ezra's return to Jerusalem with Torah scroll; public reading and reform (Ezra 7-10) | Torah becomes the constitutional document of the restored community. Likely period of Torah's final redaction. |
| **445 BCE** | Nehemiah rebuilds Jerusalem walls; social and religious reforms (Neh 1-13) | Establishes Jerusalem as a functioning polity. Mixed-marriage controversy (Neh 13:23-27) shapes later identity boundaries. |
| **~400 BCE** | Approximate end of Hebrew Bible composition (Chronicles, Ezra-Nehemiah, Malachi) | The prophetic period (Nevi'im) closes. From here, Second Temple literature is contextual, not canonical in the same sense. |
| **332 BCE** | Alexander the Great conquers Judea; beginning of Hellenistic influence | Greek language and culture begin penetrating Jewish life. Sets up the tension that defines the entire Second Temple period: accommodation vs. resistance. |
| **~300-250 BCE** | Ptolemaic control of Judea (Egypt-based); relatively tolerant | Jewish community in Alexandria grows. Joseph and Asenath, some wisdom literature from this milieu. |
| **~250-150 BCE** | Septuagint (LXX) translation in Alexandria | Torah translated into Greek; later other books follow. LXX becomes the Bible of diaspora Judaism and early Christianity. Key translation choices (almah→parthenos in Isa 7:14) shape later theology. |
| **198 BCE** | Seleucid (Syria-based) control of Judea after Battle of Panium | New overlords; increasing pressure toward Hellenization. |
| **175-164 BCE** | Antiochus IV Epiphanes; forced Hellenization; Temple desecrated (167 BCE) | "Abomination of desolation" (Dan 11:31, 12:11). Triggers the Maccabean revolt. Daniel 7-12 likely reaches final form during this crisis. |
| **164 BCE** | Maccabean revolt succeeds; Temple rededicated (Hanukkah) | 1-2 Maccabees record this period. Hanukkah is post-biblical (not in Torah). Yeshua attended it (John 10:22). |
| **152 BCE** | Jonathan assumes High Priesthood; Hasmonean dynasty begins | Non-Zadokite claiming the priesthood. This likely triggers the Qumran community's separation -- they considered Hasmonean priests illegitimate. |
| **~150-100 BCE** | Qumran community established; Dead Sea Scrolls begin to be produced | 1QS (Community Rule), Damascus Document, pesharim. "Teacher of Righteousness" vs. "Wicked Priest." Alternative Jewish community with its own calendar, purity rules, and eschatology. |
| **~150 BCE** | 4QMMT composed (Miqsat Ma'asei Ha-Torah) | Contains the phrase ma'asei ha-torah (works of the law) -- exact Hebrew equivalent of Paul's erga nomou. Shows Paul's debate language is rooted in Second Temple halakhic disputes, not abstract faith-vs-works. |
| **104-76 BCE** | Alexander Jannaeus; Hasmonean kingdom at greatest extent; crucifies 800 Pharisees | Violence between priest-kings and Pharisees. Background to Pharisaic political stance in the Gospels. |
| **63 BCE** | Pompey conquers Jerusalem; Roman control begins | Pompey enters the Holy of Holies -- finds it empty (no Ark). Judea becomes a Roman client state. Loss of political sovereignty. |
| **37-4 BCE** | Herod the Great rules as Roman client king | Massive Temple renovation (begun ~20 BCE, not completed until 63 CE -- 7 years before destruction). Herod is an Idumean, not from David's line. Slaughter of innocents (Matt 2:16) fits his documented paranoia (Josephus Ant. 15-17). |
| **~20 BCE** | Herod begins Temple expansion | The Temple Yeshua knew was Herod's magnificent structure, not the modest Second Temple of 516 BCE. "46 years to build" (John 2:20). |
| **~6-4 BCE** | Yeshua born in Bethlehem | Before Herod's death (4 BCE). The "year zero" error is a 6th-c. calculation mistake by Dionysius Exiguus. |
| **4 BCE** | Herod dies; kingdom split among sons (Archelaus, Antipas, Philip) | Archelaus so brutal Rome removes him (6 CE), placing Judea under direct Roman prefect rule. Antipas rules Galilee through Yeshua's lifetime. |
| **6 CE** | Judea becomes Roman province; census (cf. Luke 2:1-2); Judas the Galilean revolt | Direct Roman taxation triggers resistance movements. Josephus identifies this as origin of the "Fourth Philosophy" (zealot ideology). |
| **~6 CE** | Annas appointed High Priest (serves until ~15 CE; retains influence) | Father-in-law of Caiaphas. Still called "high priest" during Yeshua's trial (Luke 3:2; John 18:13), reflecting his enduring power behind the scenes. |
| **18-36 CE** | Caiaphas serves as High Priest | Ossuary discovered 1990 in Jerusalem confirms his historical existence. Presides over Yeshua's trial. |
| **26-36 CE** | Pontius Pilate serves as Roman prefect of Judea | Pilate Stone discovered 1961 at Caesarea confirms his title and dates. Known for provocations against Jewish sensibilities (Josephus Ant. 18.3.1-2). |
| **~27-30 CE** | Yeshua's public ministry | Within Second Temple Judaism: synagogue teaching, Torah interpretation, Pharisaic debate, Temple participation. All four Gospels place him firmly inside Jewish life. |
| **~30-33 CE** | Yeshua's crucifixion and resurrection | During Pesach (Passover). Connects to Leviticus 23 festival cycle: Pesach (sacrifice), Matzot (unleavened bread), Bikkurim (firstfruits). Paul: "Christ our pesach has been sacrificed" (1 Cor 5:7). |
| **~33-49 CE** | Early Jerusalem community; Yeshua's followers remain within Judaism | They worship at the Temple (Acts 2:46, 3:1), keep Torah, observe Shabbat. James (Ya'akov) leads the Jerusalem community as a Torah-observant Jew. |
| **~37 CE** | Paul's conversion | Pharisee (Phil 3:5) who becomes apostle to the Gentiles. His letters (49-60s CE) are the earliest NT documents. |
| **~40-50s CE** | Philo of Alexandria active (dies ~50 CE) | Jewish philosopher who interpreted Torah through Greek philosophical categories. His logos concept shows how Hellenistic Jews bridged Hebrew davar and Greek philosophical vocabulary. |
| **49 CE** | Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15) | Debate: must Gentile believers observe Torah? Decision: Gentiles need not be circumcised but should observe Noahide-like minimum requirements (Acts 15:20). This is an intra-Jewish halakhic ruling, not a break with Judaism. |
| **~50-60s CE** | Paul's letters written | Romans, Galatians, Corinthians, etc. All written BEFORE the Gospels. Paul's erga nomou debate is about Gentile entry requirements, not faith-vs-works in the abstract. |
| **62 CE** | James (Ya'akov) executed by Sanhedrin; Ananus ben Ananus overreaches | Josephus reports (Ant. 20.9.1) that Ananus convened Sanhedrin illegally. Public outrage forced his removal. Shows James was respected beyond the Yeshua-movement. |
| **~65-70 CE** | Gospel of Mark written (earliest Gospel; majority scholarly dating) | Written near or during the Jewish revolt. Mark 13 (the "Little Apocalypse") reflects crisis-era urgency. |
| **66 CE** | Jewish revolt against Rome begins | Multiple factions: Zealots, Sicarii, priestly aristocracy, Pharisees. Not a unified movement. Josephus is our primary source (Jewish War). |
| **68 CE** | Qumran community destroyed by Romans | Dead Sea Scrolls hidden in caves before destruction. Not rediscovered until 1947. |
| **70 CE** | Roman destruction of Second Temple | End of the sacrificial system, priesthood, and Sadducean power. Judaism reorganizes around synagogue, Torah study, and rabbinic authority (Yavneh/Jamnia). Christianity and Rabbinic Judaism both emerge from this catastrophe as distinct movements. |

---

## Key Implications for Interpretation

1. **Everything Yeshua said and did occurred within a functioning Temple system.** The sacrifices, priesthood, and festivals were active realities, not historical memories.

2. **The Pharisees were not "the bad guys."** They were a reform movement dedicated to extending Temple-level purity into everyday life. Yeshua's debates with them are intra-Jewish arguments, not Christianity-vs-Judaism.

3. **Paul wrote before the Gospels.** His letters (50s-60s CE) reflect a period when the Temple still stood and Torah observance was the norm for Jewish believers.

4. **The destruction of 70 CE changed everything.** Texts written after 70 CE (Matthew, Luke, John, Hebrews) reflect the post-Temple crisis. Reading them without this context misses their urgency.

5. **Second Temple Judaism was diverse.** Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, Zealots, Hellenizers, apocalypticists -- there was no single "Judaism" that Yeshua either affirmed or rejected.
