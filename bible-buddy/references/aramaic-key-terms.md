# Aramaic Key Terms: Verified Word Studies

Aramaic was the spoken language of first-century Palestine. Yeshua taught in Aramaic; the Gospels preserve several of his original Aramaic words. Daniel 2:4b-7:28 and Ezra 4:8-6:18 are written in Aramaic. The Targumim (Aramaic translations of Torah read in synagogues) and much of the Talmud are Aramaic.

---

| Aramaic | Transliteration | Meaning (lexicon) | Common Mistranslation | NT / Tanakh Reference |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **אַבָּא** | Abba | Father; intimate but respectful address to one's father (NOT baby-talk "Daddy") | "Daddy" (overly casual) -- Abba was used by adult children addressing their father with closeness and respect. Joachim Jeremias popularized the "Daddy" gloss (1966), but subsequent scholarship (James Barr, 1988) corrected this: Abba is the definite form "the father" used in both intimate and formal contexts. Mishnah: disciples addressed their rabbi as Abba. | Mark 14:36; Rom 8:15; Gal 4:6 |
| **טַלְיְתָא קוּמִי** | Talitha qumi | Little girl, arise; talitha = young girl/lamb, qumi = arise (feminine imperative) | "Magic incantation" -- Mark 5:41 preserves Yeshua's original Aramaic words to Jairus's daughter. This is a simple Aramaic command, not a spell. Mark translates it for his Greek audience: to korasion, soi lego, egeire. The preservation of the original language reflects eyewitness memory. | Mark 5:41 |
| **אֵלִי אֵלִי לְמָא שְׁבַקְתָּנִי** | Eli Eli lema sabachthani | My God, my God, why have you forsaken me; Psalm 22:1 in Aramaic (Matthew) or mixed Hebrew-Aramaic (Mark: Eloi) | "Cry of despair / loss of faith" -- in first-century Jewish practice, quoting the opening line of a psalm invoked the entire psalm. Psalm 22 moves from lament (vv.1-21) to vindication and universal worship (vv.22-31). The cry signals the whole psalm's narrative arc, not isolated despair. | Matt 27:46; Mark 15:34 (cf. Ps 22:1) |
| **מָרָנָא תָא** | Maranatha | Our Lord, come!; maran = our Lord, atha = come (imperative) | Untranslated liturgical word -- 1 Cor 16:22 preserves this Aramaic prayer, proving the earliest (Aramaic-speaking, Jewish) believers already addressed Yeshua as maran (Lord). This is significant because maran/mare is the Aramaic equivalent of Hebrew adon, used for God in the Targumim. | 1 Cor 16:22; Didache 10:6 |
| **אֶפַּתָּא** | Ephphatha | Be opened; ethpaal imperative of petach (to open) | "Magic formula" -- Mark 7:34 preserves Yeshua's Aramaic command to a deaf man. The ethpaal (reflexive-passive) form means "be yourself opened." Like talitha qumi, Mark translates for Greek readers: dianoichtheti. | Mark 7:34 |
| **רָקָא** | Raqa | Empty-headed, worthless, senseless; a term of contempt | Generic "insult" -- Matt 5:22 places raqa on a sliding scale of severity: anger → raqa (contempt for intellect) → moros (fool, contempt for character). Raqa appears in rabbinic literature as a common Aramaic insult. Yeshua teaches that verbal contempt is as serious as physical violence. | Matt 5:22 |
| **מָמוֹנָא** | Mamona | Wealth, property, money; possibly from Aramaic root '-m-n (to trust/entrust) | "Demon of greed" -- mamona is not a deity or demon name. It is a common Aramaic word for money/wealth found in the Targumim and rabbinic texts (e.g., Targum Onkelos on Deut 6:5). Yeshua personifies mamona in Matt 6:24 / Luke 16:13 as a rhetorical device: you cannot serve God AND wealth. Medieval tradition incorrectly identified Mammon as a specific demon. | Matt 6:24; Luke 16:9-13 |
| **קָרְבָּנָא** | Qorbana | Offering, gift dedicated to God; from q-r-b (to draw near/approach) | "Temple donation" in modern financial sense -- Mark 7:11 preserves the Aramaic corban in Yeshua's critique: declaring property as qorban (dedicated to the Temple) to avoid supporting elderly parents. Yeshua argues this practice violates the Torah command to honor (kabbed) parents (Exod 20:12), using human tradition to nullify God's word. | Mark 7:11 (cf. Lev 1:2; Mishnah Nedarim) |
| **גָּלְגֹּלְתָּא** | Gulgolta | Skull; a place name from the Aramaic word for skull | -- The Gospels preserve the Aramaic place name Golgotha (Matt 27:33, Mark 15:22, John 19:17), translated as Kranion (Greek) and Calvaria (Latin Vulgate → English "Calvary"). The name describes the location's appearance, not a theological concept. | Matt 27:33; Mark 15:22; John 19:17 |
| **כֵּיפָא** | Kepha | Rock, stone; Yeshua's Aramaic nickname for Simon | "Peter" as a proper name -- the Greek Petros is a translation of Aramaic Kepha, not an independent name. In Aramaic kepha means rock/stone. Paul uses the Aramaic form Kephas 8 times (Gal 1:18, 2:9, etc.), showing early communities used the original Aramaic. Matt 16:18 wordplay works in Aramaic: "You are Kepha, and on this kepha I will build." | John 1:42; 1 Cor 1:12, 3:22, 15:5; Gal 1:18, 2:9 |
| **רַבּוּנִי** | Rabbouni | My master, my great teacher; heightened form of rabbi (rav + suffix -oni) | "Rabbi" (generic) -- rabbouni is a more personal, intensified form than rabbi. John 20:16 preserves Mary Magdalene's Aramaic address to the risen Yeshua. The Gospel translates it as didaskalos (teacher), but the Aramaic carries deeper respect and personal relationship than the Greek can convey. | Mark 10:51; John 20:16 |
| **בַּר** | Bar | Son; Aramaic equivalent of Hebrew ben | -- Bar appears throughout NT names: Bar-Abbas (son of the father), Bar-Timaios (son of Timaios), Bar-Yona (son of Jonah, Matt 16:17), Bar-Nabba (son of encouragement = Barnabas). Daniel 7:13 bar enash (son of man) is the Aramaic equivalent of Hebrew ben adam. | Dan 7:13; Matt 16:17; Acts 4:36 |
| **בַּר אֱנָשׁ** | Bar enash | Son of man; human being, mortal (generic), or the specific figure of Daniel 7:13 | "Divine title" exclusively -- bar enash in Aramaic simply means "a human being." In Daniel 7:13, one "like a bar enash" receives dominion from the Ancient of Days. The term operates on two levels: ordinary meaning (human) and Daniel's specific visionary figure. Yeshua's self-designation draws on this productive ambiguity. 1 Enoch 46-48 and 4 Ezra 13 show the "Son of Man" figure was already developing in Second Temple Judaism. | Dan 7:13; 1 Enoch 46:1-4; 4 Ezra 13 |
| **רָז** | Raz | Mystery, secret; a hidden divine plan revealed to the elect | Gnostic "secret knowledge" -- raz in Daniel 2:18-19, 27-30 refers to God's concealed plan for history, revealed through interpretation (pesher). The Dead Sea Scrolls use raz nihyeh (mystery of what is to be) extensively (1QpHab 7:1-5; 1Q27). Paul's Greek mysterion (Rom 16:25; 1 Cor 2:7) directly translates raz: God's covenant plan now disclosed, not esoteric initiation knowledge. | Dan 2:18-19, 27-30; 1QpHab 7; 1Q27 |
| **פְּשַׁר** | Pesher | Interpretation; a method of reading scripture as fulfilled in the present community | "Commentary" (generic) -- pesher is a specific interpretive method found in the Dead Sea Scrolls (1QpHab, 4QpNah, 4QpPs37): scripture has a hidden meaning (raz) that applies to the community's own time. Daniel 2-5 uses pesher for dream interpretation. This "this is that" method (scripture fulfilled NOW) appears in Acts 2:16 (Peter: "this is what was spoken by Joel") and Matt 1:22-23 ("to fulfill what was spoken"). | Dan 2:4-5:26; 1QpHab; Acts 2:16 |
| **הוֹשַׁעְנָא** | Hoshana | Save, please!; from Hebrew hoshi'ah na (Ps 118:25), Aramaicized form | "Praise word" / generic cheer -- hoshana is a prayer of urgent petition, not a praise shout. Psalm 118:25-26 is a Sukkot (Tabernacles) liturgical text. The crowd's shout at Yeshua's entry (Mark 11:9-10) carries messianic-political overtones: "Save us NOW, Son of David!" This is a petition for liberation, not a worship chorus. | Mark 11:9-10; John 12:13 (cf. Ps 118:25-26) |
