# Hebrew Key Terms: Verified Word Studies from Test Responses

All entries below were extracted from test response files where Hebrew terms were analyzed with root/lexicon data and verified via scripture fetching (Sefaria) or WebSearch.

---

| Hebrew | Root | Transliteration | Meaning (lexicon) | Common Mistranslation | Verified In |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **אֶחָד** | א-ח-ד | echad | Cardinal number "one"; can express unified oneness (Gen 2:24 "one flesh") | "Compound unity" -- a theological argument not supported by standard Hebrew lexicography (BDB, HALOT). The compound-unity apologetic became popular in 20th-century evangelical/Messianic circles. | fact-check Claim 2; folk-religion, modern-judaism |
| **עַלְמָה** | ע-ל-מ | almah | Young woman of marriageable age; from root meaning "hidden/concealed" | "Virgin" -- Hebrew has a specific word for virgin: betulah. LXX translated almah as parthenos, and Chinese RCUV follows with "童女." Lyu Zhenzhong correctly translates "少婦" (young woman). Appears 7x in Tanakh; Prov 30:19 uses it in an explicitly sexual context. | almah-isaiah-7-14, hindu-theology |
| **בְּתוּלָה** | ב-ת-ל | betulah | Virgin; specifically a woman who has not had sexual relations | Often conflated with almah, but betulah is the precise term for virginity. Appears ~50x in Tanakh. Isaiah chose almah, not betulah, in Isa 7:14. | almah-isaiah-7-14 |
| **מָשִׁיחַ** | מ-שׁ-ח | mashiach | "Anointed one"; functional description of anyone anointed with oil for a role | "Christ" as a divine title -- in Tanakh, mashiach refers to kings (Saul, David), high priests (Lev 4:3), and even the Persian king Cyrus (Isa 45:1). Appears ~39x, never as an eschatological savior figure. The messianic specialist meaning developed in the late Second Temple period. | mashiach-concept |
| **חֶסֶד** | ח-ס-ד | hesed | Covenantal loyalty; steadfast, committed love within a covenant relationship (berith) | "Mercy" or "loving-kindness" -- these translations miss the covenantal dimension. Hesed is YHWH's faithful commitment to Israel even when Israel breaks the covenant. Not generic compassion. | guan-gong-theology, confucianism, folk-religion, church-language |
| **צְדָקָה** | צ-ד-ק | tsedaqah | Right standing within covenant relationship; being in correct relational position with YHWH | "Righteousness" as abstract moral virtue -- tsedaqah is relational and covenantal, not an independent ethical quality. In Gen 15:6, YHWH "reckons" Abraham's trust as tsedaqah. It includes social justice, charity, and covenant faithfulness. | guan-gong-theology, matthew-5-17-torah, confucianism |
| **מִשְׁפָּט** | שׁ-פ-ט | mishpat | Covenant legal justice; the process of legislation, judicial ruling, and enforcement | "Justice" in abstract sense -- mishpat specifically refers to structural, institutional justice protecting widows, orphans, and sojourners (Deut 10:18, 24:17). It is a systemic concept within the covenant framework. | guan-gong-theology, confucianism |
| **רוּחַ** | ר-ו-ח | ruach | Wind, breath, spirit; YHWH's active presence and power | "Spirit" as independent entity or Daoist "qi" -- ruach in Tanakh is always YHWH's own action/presence, not an independent being or impersonal cosmic energy. Psalm 104:29-30: YHWH sends/withdraws ruach. Gen 1:2: merachefet (hovering) implies intentional, volitional action. | daoist-theology, folk-religion, jw-theology, shinto-theology |
| **נִשְׁמָה** | נ-שׁ-מ | neshamah | Breath, breathing; specifically nose-breath | "Soul" or "spirit" in Platonic sense -- neshamah in Gen 2:7 is literal breath blown into nostrils (af). It describes biological life-giving, not a mystical energy point. CCV and Lyu translations ("有生命的人") are more accurate than RCUV "有靈的活人." | yiguandao-theology, daoist-theology |
| **נֶפֶשׁ** | נ-פ-שׁ | nefesh | Living being; whole animated creature (including blood, breath, desire, life-force) | "Soul" as immaterial entity separable from body -- nefesh chayyah in Gen 1:20-21 also describes sea creatures and animals. It means the entire living being, not a disembodied soul. | yiguandao-theology |
| **דָּבָר** | ד-ב-ר | davar | Word, speech, matter, thing; God's spoken command that carries active power | "道" (Dao) -- the Chinese translation of logos as "道" creates false association with Laozi's Dao. The Hebrew background of John's logos is davar -- YHWH speaks and things happen (Gen 1 pattern: va-yomer, "and God said"). Davar is personal, volitional, covenantal. | daoist-theology, gnostic-theology |
| **תֹהוּ וָבֹהוּ** | -- | tohu va-vohu | Formless and void; unformed, unshaped state (hendiadys) | "Chaos" with Daoist connotations -- Chinese "空虛混沌" imports Daoist associations (Zhuangzi's hundun). Tohu appears in Isa 34:11 and Jer 4:23 describing devastated land. It describes a state awaiting ordering, not philosophical nothingness or wuji. | daoist-theology |
| **תְּהוֹם** | -- | tehom | Deep, primordial waters, abyss | Cognate with Babylonian Tiamat, but Genesis deliberately demythologizes it -- tehom is not an enemy deity to be defeated, just unordered water awaiting YHWH's structuring command. The true dialogue partner is ANE creation myths (Enuma Elish), not Daodejing. | daoist-theology |
| **הָבֶל** | ה-ב-ל | hevel | Vapor, breath, transience, futility | Buddhist "emptiness" (sunyata) -- hevel in Ecclesiastes describes temporal unreliability of worldly things within a covenant-with-God framework. It is not the Buddhist ontological claim that all phenomena lack inherent existence. | buddhist-theology |
| **אהב** | א-ה-ב | ahav | To love; in Torah context, a covenant-action verb meaning to fulfill obligations faithfully | Generic emotional "love" -- in ANE treaty language (Amarna Letters), "love" = treaty loyalty. Deut 6:5 "love YHWH" = be faithful to the covenant. Lev 19:18 "love your neighbor" = fulfill covenant obligations toward them. | confucianism, folk-religion, buddhist-theology |
| **כַּבֵּד** | כ-ב-ד | kabbed | To make heavy/weighty; to honor, give weight to | "Filial piety" (孝) -- kabbed in Exod 20:12 is a covenant obligation, not a Confucian social ethic. The same root is used for "honoring YHWH" (Prov 3:9). It does not include ancestor worship, which Torah explicitly prohibits (Deut 18:10-12). | confucianism |
| **בָּחַר** | ב-ח-ר | bachar | To choose, select, elect | Individual predestination to salvation/damnation -- bachar in Deut 7:6-8 is national/covenantal election of Israel as a people for a mission, not individual eternal destiny. YHWH chose Israel not because of merit but for covenant purposes. | calvinism-theology |
| **בְּרִית** | -- | berit / b'rit | Covenant; a formal, binding agreement between YHWH and Israel | "Testament" -- the Latin testamentum (will/testament) misleads. Berit is a bilateral relationship with obligations on both sides. The "new covenant" (berit chadashah, Jer 31:31-33) puts Torah IN the heart, not abolishes it. | fact-check Claim 15; supersessionism, sola-fide |
| **בְּרִית חֲדָשָׁה** | -- | berit chadashah | New/renewed covenant | "New Testament that replaces the old" -- Jer 31:33 explicitly says "I will put my Torah within them, and on their hearts I will write it." The "newness" is the mode of delivery (internal vs. external), not the replacement of Torah content. Chadashah can mean "renewed" (cf. chodesh = new moon, same moon each month). | fact-check Claim 15 |
| **הַרְפוּ** | ר-פ-ה | harpu (Hifil imperative of raphah) | Stop, cease striving, let go, slacken | "Be still" as quiet meditation -- in the military context of Psalm 46 (wars, nations in uproar, bows broken, spears shattered), harpu means "cease fighting / stop striving." Raphah means to slacken or let go. "Drop your weapons" is a vivid but loose paraphrase. | fact-check Claim 9 |
| **מַעֲשֵׂי הַתּוֹרָה** | -- | ma'asei ha-torah | Works/deeds of the Torah; specific halakhic rulings | "Works of the law" as generic legalism -- Dead Sea Scroll 4QMMT (Miqsat Ma'asei Ha-Torah, c. 150 BCE) contains this exact phrase, referring to specific purity rulings about the Temple. Paul's Greek erga nomou is the direct equivalent. The phrase appears only in 4QMMT and Paul's letters. Scholar Martin Abegg first publicized this connection. | fact-check Claim 10; sola-fide |
| **קָרָא בְשֵׁם** | ק-ר-א | qara' b'shem | To call upon the name; to invoke within covenant relationship | Ritual incantation / mantra -- in first-century Judaism, "calling on YHWH's name" meant acknowledging YHWH as covenant God and responding in faithfulness, not a spell-like utterance. Joel 3:5 is bilateral: humans call on YHWH, and YHWH calls the remnant. | japanese-buddhism |
| **שֵׁם** | שׁ-מ | shem | Name; represents character, actions, and covenant commitments of a person | Magical syllable sequence -- in first-century Jewish context, a "name" represents the whole person's identity and authority. "In the name of" = with the authorization of. Not the phonetic sequence itself having power. | japanese-buddhism |
| **מָשָׁל** | מ-שׁ-ל | mashal | Proverb, parable, riddle, allegory, taunt; a rhetorical tool with a clear referent | Zen koan -- mashal always points to a specific historical situation, covenant obligation, or moral lesson. Yeshua explained his parables to disciples (Mark 4:34). A mashal has answers; a koan deliberately resists conceptual resolution. | japanese-buddhism |
| **פֶּסַח** | פ-ס-ח | pesach | Passover; from the verb "to pass over / skip over" | "Easter" -- the resurrection was understood within the Pesach-Matzot-Bikkurim festival cycle (Lev 23). Paul calls Christ "our Passover lamb" (1 Cor 5:7). The Greek pascha directly transliterates Aramaic pasha from Hebrew pesach. | easter-theology |
| **עֹמֶר / רֵאשִׁית** | -- | omer / reshit | Sheaf; firstfruits (first of the barley harvest) | Unrelated to resurrection -- Paul explicitly uses aparche (firstfruits, 1 Cor 15:20) to describe the resurrection, connecting it to the Lev 23:10 firstfruits offering during Passover week. | easter-theology |
| **יִרְאַת יְהוָה** | י-ר-א | yir'at YHWH | Fear/awe of YHWH; reverent submission to the covenant God | Generic wisdom or moral reverence -- Prov 1:7 and 9:10 declare that without yir'at YHWH, there is no true wisdom. This is not "adding a religious element" to wisdom; it is the ontological foundation. Confucian wisdom requires no deity. | confucianism |
| **מוּסָר** | י-ס-ר | musar | Discipline, correction, instruction (includes painful learning) | "Education" -- musar contains "chastisement" (Prov 3:11-12: "YHWH disciplines those he loves"). It is covenantal discipline, not self-directed cultivation. | confucianism |
| **בְּרָכָה** | ב-ר-כ | berakhah | Blessing; YHWH's provision within the covenant framework | "Divine blessing" as transactional favor -- berakhah is tied to Torah observance (Deut 28:1-14), not to offerings that manipulate a deity. Micah 6:6-7 explicitly rejects transactional religion. | guan-gong-theology |
| **עֶצֶב** | ע-צ-ב | etzev | Pain, toil, grief (the curse-toil of Gen 3:16-17) | Generic "sorrow" -- Prov 10:22 says YHWH's blessing "adds no etzev." This echoes the Genesis 3 curse, contrasting God's blessing with the curse of human toil. | guan-gong-theology |
| **אֱמוּנָה** | א-מ-ן | emunah | Faithfulness, trust, reliability; relational trust placed in YHWH | "Belief in propositions" -- Hebrew emunah is not Greek-style propositional belief. It means placing one's full weight on the other party. Abraham's he'emin (Gen 15:6) was relational trust in YHWH's promise, not intellectual assent. | guan-gong-theology, sola-fide |
| **עֵץ** | -- | etz | Tree, wood; does not specify shape | "Cross" specifically -- etz in Deut 21:22 means "wood/tree." The verb talah means "hang." Neither word determines the shape of the execution device. The shape question is resolved by archaeology (Giv'at ha-Mivtar) and Greek textual evidence (plural helown "nails" in John 20:25), not by the Hebrew word itself. | jw-theology |
| **נקם / נטר** | נ-ק-מ / נ-ט-ר | naqam / natar | Avenge, take revenge / hold a grudge, bear resentment | -- | Torah (Lev 19:18) explicitly forbids both vengeance and grudge-holding. Yeshua's "turn the other cheek" teaching is an expansion of this Torah command, not a Buddhist import. | buddhist-theology |
| **שֹׂנֵא** | שׂ-נ-א | sone | One who hates; enemy | -- | Prov 25:21-22 commands feeding your enemy (sone). Exod 23:4-5 commands helping your enemy's donkey. These predate Buddhism by centuries. | buddhist-theology |
| **גֵּר** | -- | ger | Sojourner, foreigner, resident alien | -- | Lev 19:34 commands "love the ger as yourself" (ve-ahavta lo kamokha), with the specific motivation: "because you were gerim in Egypt." This empathy-from-shared-experience ethic is unique to Torah. | buddhist-theology, confucianism |
| **אָסַר / הִתִּיר** | א-ס-ר / ה-ת-ר | asar / hitir | Bind (prohibit) / loose (permit); halakhic rulings on Torah practice | "Spiritual warfare binding/loosing" — these are standard rabbinic legal terms. When Yeshua grants Peter authority to "bind and loose" (Matt 16:19), he is delegating halakhic ruling authority, not demon-binding power. Mishnah uses this terminology extensively. | charismatic-theology |
| **סְמִיכָה** | ס-מ-כ | semikhah | Laying on of hands; authorization and commissioning for a role | "Power transfer / impartation" — in first-century Judaism, semikhah was the act of authorizing someone for leadership (cf. Moses → Joshua, Num 27:18-23). It transmitted authority and responsibility, not a transferable spiritual substance. | charismatic-theology |
| **שָׁלִיחַ** | שׁ-ל-ח | shaliach | Commissioned agent; one sent with authority for a specific task | "Permanent hierarchical office" — a shaliach acts on behalf of the sender for a defined mission. "A person's shaliach is as himself" (Mishnah Berakhot 5:5). The role is task-based and temporary, not a permanent rank in a governance hierarchy. Greek equivalent: apostolos. | charismatic-theology |
