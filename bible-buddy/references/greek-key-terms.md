# Greek Key Terms: Verified Word Studies from Test Responses

All entries below were extracted from test response files where Greek NT terms were analyzed with lexical data and verified via scripture fetching or WebSearch.

---

| Greek | Root | Transliteration | Meaning (lexicon) | Common Mistranslation | Verified In |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **ἓν** (neuter) | εἷς | hen | One (neuter gender); unity of nature, essence, or purpose -- NOT personal identity | "I and the Father are the same person" -- John 10:30 uses neuter hen, not masculine heis. If Jesus meant personal identity with the Father, heis would be expected. The neuter rules out Sabellianism (same person) but the exact nature of the unity (purpose, essence, or both) is debated. Same neuter hen in 1 Cor 3:8 for cooperative purpose. | fact-check Claim 3 |
| **εἷς** (masculine) | εἷς | heis | One (masculine gender); one person, one individual | -- | Contrasted with hen above. Mark 12:29 uses heis echoing the Shema. The distinction between heis (personal identity) and hen (unity) is grammatically significant in John 10:30. | fact-check Claim 3; folk-religion |
| **λόγος** | λέγω | logos | Word, speech, reason, account; in Johannine usage, the creative and revelatory word of God | Chinese "道" (Dao) -- the 1919 Union Version chose "道" to translate logos, creating false association with Laozi's Dao. The Hebrew background is davar (YHWH's spoken command) and hokhmah (Wisdom, Prov 8:22-31). Even Philo of Alexandria (~20 BCE-50 CE) kept logos within Jewish monotheism. | daoist-theology, gnostic-theology, jw-theology |
| **σάρξ** | -- | sarx | Flesh; physical body, human nature in its most material sense | -- | John 1:14 "ho logos sarx egeneto" (the Word became flesh) uses the most material Greek term for body. This directly contradicts Gnostic Docetism, which taught the divine could never truly enter matter. | gnostic-theology |
| **θεός** (anarthrous, pre-verbal) | -- | theos | God; when anarthrous and before the verb, qualitative usage describing nature/essence | "A god" (indefinite) -- NWT translates John 1:1c as "a god," but Colwell's Rule (1933) and Harner (1973) show this is qualitative: "the Word had the nature of God." If John meant "a god," he would use heis theos or theos tis. Introducing a secondary deity contradicts the Shema (Deut 6:4) that every first-century Jew recited daily. | jw-theology |
| **κεχαριτωμένη** | χαριτόω | kecharitomene | Having been graced; passive perfect participle meaning "one who has been endowed with grace" | "Full of grace" (gratia plena) -- the Vulgate's translation shifts to active voice, as if grace is Mary's inherent quality. Greek is PASSIVE: she received grace from God. Same root charitoo in Eph 1:6 applies to ALL believers "graced in the Beloved." | catholic-theology |
| **ἕως οὗ** | -- | heos hou | Until; natural implication: the state described changed after the point in time | -- | Matt 1:25: Joseph "did not know her until (heos hou) she bore a son." The natural Greek reading implies consummation after birth. Used in Catholic perpetual virginity debate. | catholic-theology |
| **ἀδελφός** vs. **ἀνεψιός** | -- | adelphos / anepsios | Brother (biological) vs. cousin | "Jesus' brothers were cousins" -- Greek has a specific word for cousin (anepsios), used by Paul in Col 4:10 for Barnabas' cousin Mark. Every NT reference to Jesus' siblings uses adelphos, and places them alongside "his mother Mary" (Matt 13:55-56). | catholic-theology |
| **καινός** vs. **νέος** | -- | kainos / neos | Kainos: new in quality/kind, fresh, novel. Neos: new in time, recent, young | Kainos as "renewed/refreshed" -- Heb 8:13 uses kainos (not neos). Standard lexicons (BDAG, Thayer) define kainos as "new, unused, previously unknown" -- qualitatively different and superior. Glossing it as merely "renewed" imports a theological conclusion (covenant renewed, not replaced) into the lexical definition. Notably, Heb 12:24 DOES use neos, showing the author knew both words. | fact-check Claim 8 |
| **πίστις** | πείθω | pistis | Faith, trust, faithfulness, reliability; corresponds to Hebrew emunah | "Intellectual belief" or "faith alone vs. works" -- pistis in first-century Jewish usage (corresponding to Hebrew emunah) means relational trust and covenant faithfulness. Paul uses pistis Iesou Christou (Rom 3:22), which can be read as "faithfulness OF Jesus" (subjective genitive) or "faith IN Jesus" (objective genitive). James 2:22 says pistis "worked together with" (synergei) Abraham's works. | sola-fide |
| **ἔργα νόμου** | -- | erga nomou | Works of the law; specific Torah-observance practices that serve as Jewish identity markers | "Good works / moral effort" -- erga nomou corresponds exactly to Hebrew ma'asei ha-torah found in Dead Sea Scroll 4QMMT (c. 150 BCE), referring to specific halakhic rulings about Temple purity. Paul's debate was about whether Gentiles must adopt Jewish identity markers (circumcision, dietary laws, Sabbath) to join the covenant, not about moral effort vs. grace in the abstract. | fact-check Claim 10; sola-fide |
| **δικαιοσύνη** | δίκη | dikaiosyne | Righteousness, justice; covenant-right-standing | "Forensic imputed righteousness" (Luther's framework) -- in first-century Jewish usage, dikaiosyne translates Hebrew tsedaqah: living rightly within covenant relationship. It includes justice, charity, integrity, faithfulness to God and fairness to people. Not merely a legal verdict. | matthew-5-17-torah, sola-fide |
| **καταλύω** | -- | katalyo | To tear down, dissolve, destroy; in rabbinic background corresponds to Hebrew batel -- to invalidate a Torah interpretation | "Abolish" in the sense of ending Torah -- in rabbinic debate, "destroying" Torah means giving an interpretation that renders a commandment unobservable. Jesus in Matt 5:17 says "I came not to katalysai but to plerosai." | matthew-5-17-torah |
| **πληρόω** | -- | pleroo | To fill up, make full, complete; in rabbinic background corresponds to Hebrew qiyyem -- to establish, correctly interpret | "Fulfill and thereby end" -- this common Christian reading ("Jesus fulfilled the law so we don't have to keep it") is a serious post-hoc theological misread. First-century meaning: "correctly interpret and fully practice." Isa 42:21: YHWH "magnifies Torah" (yagdil Torah). The antonym pair batel/qiyyem (destroy/establish Torah) maps exactly onto katalysai/plerosai. | matthew-5-17-torah |
| **ἰῶτα / κεραία** | -- | iota / keraia | Iota: smallest Hebrew letter yod (י). Keraia: decorative stroke/serif (tagin) on Hebrew letters | -- | Matt 5:18: "not one iota or one keraia will pass from the law." This rabbinic metaphor (cf. Talmud Menahot 29b -- Moses seeing God adding crowns/tagin to letters) affirms that even the smallest detail of Torah remains valid. | matthew-5-17-torah |
| **παράκλητος** | -- | parakletos | Advocate, legal defender, one called alongside to help; courtroom/bet din term | "Comforter" as generic spiritual presence -- in first-century Jewish legal context (bet din), parakletos is a legal advocate who defends you in court. John 14:16 describes the Holy Spirit's function within covenantal framework, not a universal compassion figure. | folk-religion |
| **πνεῦμα** | -- | pneuma | Spirit, breath, wind; Greek equivalent of Hebrew ruach | Impersonal force/energy -- Acts 5:3-4 equates "lying to the Holy Spirit" with "lying to God" (you cannot deceive impersonal electricity). Rom 8:26-27 attributes personal actions to pneuma: interceding (hyperentygchanei), having a mind (phronema). These are actions requiring personhood. | jw-theology |
| **σταυρός** | -- | stauros | In classical Greek: upright stake. By first-century Roman usage: execution structure of various shapes including cross-beam types | "Torture stake" (single upright pole only) -- by Seneca's time (~4 BCE-65 CE), stauros covered multiple Roman execution structures. John 20:25 uses plural helown (nails), implying hands were separately fastened (consistent with cross-beam). Archaeological evidence: Yehohanan's heel bone (Giv'at ha-Mivtar, 1968, Jerusalem). Staurogram in earliest papyri P66/P75 (~200 CE) depicts a figure on a cross-beam structure. | jw-theology |
| **ἥλων** (plural) | ἧλος | helown | Nails (plural) | -- | John 20:25: Thomas says "the marks of the nails" (typon ton helown). Plural "nails" implies both hands were separately fastened, inconsistent with overlapped-hands single-stake theory. | jw-theology |
| **μονογενής** | μόνος + γένος | monogenes | Unique, one-of-a-kind; NOT "only begotten" | "Only begotten" (KJV) -- monogenes means "one and only, unique," not "generated/born." In LXX it translates Hebrew yachid (Gen 22:2, Abraham's "unique" son Isaac). The "begotten" reading comes from conflation with gennao (to beget). | church-language |
| **ἱλαστήριον** | -- | hilasterion | Mercy seat (kapporet); place of atonement/propitiation | "Propitiation" in the narrow sense of appeasing divine wrath -- in LXX, hilasterion translates Hebrew kapporet (the mercy seat / lid of the Ark, Lev 16:2). Paul uses it in Rom 3:25, connecting Jesus' role to the Yom Kippur atonement ritual, not to a pagan wrath-appeasement framework. | sola-fide |
| **Ἰουδαΐζειν** | -- | Ioudaizein | To live like a Jew; to adopt Jewish customs (circumcision, dietary laws, etc.) | -- | Gal 2:14: Paul accuses Peter of compelling Gentiles to Ioudaizein. This verb identifies the specific debate: whether Gentiles must adopt Jewish identity markers, not whether Torah itself is valid. | sola-fide |
| **ἀμεταμέλητα** | -- | ametameleta | Irrevocable, without regret, cannot be taken back | -- | Rom 11:29: "The gifts and calling of God are irrevocable (ametameleta)." Paul's climactic statement that God's election of Israel is permanent -- directly contradicting supersessionism/replacement theology. | supersessionism |
| **ἀγριέλαιος** | -- | agrielaios | Wild olive (tree/branch) | -- | Rom 11:17: Gentile believers are wild olive branches grafted INTO Israel's cultivated olive tree. The direction is Gentiles joining Israel's root, not Israel being replaced by the Church. "The root supports you, not you the root" (11:18). | supersessionism |
| **γλῶσσα / διάλεκτος** | -- | glossa / dialektos | Tongue, language / dialect, regional language | "Unintelligible ecstatic speech" -- Acts 2:4 uses glossa but 2:6-8 clarifies with dialektos: each person heard in their own regional language. The Pentecost event was a cross-linguistic communication miracle (paralleling the rabbinic tradition of YHWH's voice splitting into 70 languages at Sinai), not unintelligible vocalization. | charismatic-theology, true-jesus-church |
| **ἐγώ εἰμί** | -- | ego eimi | "I am"; in John 8:58 echoes the divine name ehyeh (Exod 3:14) | -- | John 8:58: "Before Abraham was, I am (ego eimi)." The crowd's immediate attempt to stone Jesus (8:59) shows they understood this as a divine self-identification claim echoing YHWH's self-revelation at the burning bush. | jw-theology, gnostic-theology |
| **ἀπαρχή** | -- | aparche | Firstfruits; the first portion of the harvest offered to God | -- | 1 Cor 15:20: Christ as aparche of those who have fallen asleep. Paul directly connects the resurrection to the Leviticus 23:10 firstfruits offering (reshit qetzirekhem) -- the sheaf of barley waved before YHWH during Passover week. The resurrection IS the firstfruits festival. | easter-theology |
| **μὴ γένοιτο** | -- | me genoito | "May it never be!"; strongest possible Greek negation | -- | Rom 11:1 "Has God rejected his people? Me genoito!" and Rom 3:31 "Do we nullify the law through faith? Me genoito!" Paul uses this emphatic denial to reject both supersessionism and antinomianism. | supersessionism, sola-fide |
| **κοινωνοὶ θείας φύσεως** | -- | koinonoi theias physeos | Sharers/participants of the divine nature | "Humans become God in essence" -- 2 Pet 1:4 in first-century Jewish context most likely means restoring/living out the tselem (image of God) through ethical transformation within covenant. Not ontological fusion with God (which would violate Isa 40:18, 25 -- the absolute Creator-creature distinction). Systematic theosis doctrine developed in 4th century. | orthodox-theology, local-church |
